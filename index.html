Lightweight 3DGS:
Physical Constraint Pruning via Spatio-Scale Dual Priors

Report Code

Achieving highly pure 3D geometric topology and significant storage reduction by eliminating over 1.3 million floating artifacts in unbounded scenes.

Abstract

While 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) demonstrates exceptional rendering efficiency in novel view synthesis tasks, its underlying optimization mechanism exhibits significant geometric flaws in unconstrained real-world mapping. 3DGS tends to generate a massive amount of redundant, highly elongated Gaussian ellipsoids (floating and specular artifacts) in blank areas. This not only leads to excessive parameter bloat and inefficient memory usage but also causes severe physical geometric distortions.

This study proposes a computationally lightweight and efficient Spatio-Scale Dual Distance-Aware Regularization strategy. Without altering the underlying CUDA rasterization architecture of 3DGS, it achieves a paradigm shift from "pure photometric fitting" to "physical geometric fidelity" by introducing spatial and scale priors. Experiments demonstrate that our method reduces model storage by 18.9% in synthetic scenes and achieves a 26.2% volume compression in large-scale unbounded real-world scenes, resulting in highly pure 3D assets suitable for memory-constrained applications.

Method

To address floating artifacts, we designed a dual prior regularization term:

  • Spatial Prior: For unbounded real-world scenes, a 3D TSDF grid is constructed to achieve ultra-fast distance querying. The spatial weight is defined as:
    $$ w_{spatial} = 1 - \exp\left(-\gamma \cdot \frac{d_i}{R_{scene}}\right) $$
  • Scale Prior: To suppress abnormally elongated Gaussian artifacts generated to fit specular edges, a local physical scale penalty is introduced:
    $$ w_{scale} = \frac{\max(s_i)}{R_{scene}} $$
  • Dual Penalty: The final loss function introduces a hard truncation mechanism, forcing Gaussians in non-entity regions to trigger the native `prune` mechanism during gradient updates:
    $$ \mathcal{L}_{opacity} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} o_i \cdot (w_{spatial} + \lambda \cdot w_{scale}) $$
